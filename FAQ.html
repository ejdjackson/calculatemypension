General Questions
▶
How does the calculator work?
There are three stages to the calculation:

1. Accumulate Funds To Retirement: Your funds are accumulated along your planned regular contributions and fund growth up to your chosen retirement date.

2. Calculate Maximum Affordable Retirment Income: Payments from your funds in retirement are then simulated multiple times, adjusting the values each time in order to target a final fund of zero. The payment that hits this target is your maximum affordable retirement income. If this is less than your desired retirement income, the shortfall is calculated.

3. Optimise Drawdown Plan for Tax Efficiency: If you have ISA funds to draw from, the split of payments between pension and ISA is then calculated each year to minimise tax due.
▶
What if there is a shortfall at my desired retirement age?

There are three main options:

- Increase your monthly contributions.

- Retire later.

- Reduce your desired retirement income.

Adjust these inputs to try to remove the shortfall.

▶
How can the calculator help me if I have already retired?

If you are already retired, the calculator can assist in managing the drawdown of your funds. The calculator provides an updated overview of how your finances are performing, helping you monitor the sustainability of your savings and income over time. It can indicate whether you can afford to increase your withdrawals to enhance your lifestyle or if you need to reduce them to ensure your funds last throughout your retirement.

▶
What if I want to retire early?

If you plan to retire before you can access your pension fund, your ISAs are automatically used to fund your retirement. Calculated pension payments will commence at the earliest allowable age.

▶
Is my data saved?
All information you enter is only saved locally within your browser so that you can come back to the calculator and not have to re-enter any figures. No data is collected or stored externally.
Your State Pension
▶
How much is my state pension?

The amount of state pension you will receive depends on your National Insurance record.

As of April 2025, the full State Pension is £11,976 per year.

To qualify for the full amount, you need 35 years of National Insurance contributions or credits.

You can get an up to date personal calculation at www.gov.uk.

The calculator assumes you will be eligible for the full state pension.

An option to input a lower amount will be added soon.

▶
At what age do I receive my state pension?

The current state pension age in the UK is 66 for both men and women. However, this is set to increase gradually:

- To age 67: This will occur for those born between 6 April 1960 and 5 March 1961, with the increase beginning in 2026 and completing in 2028​

- To age 68: Those born after April 1977 are currently expected to have a state pension age of 68, although the timing of this change could vary based on government review​.

You can get an accurate calculation of the exact age that you will receive you state pension at www.gov.uk.

The calculator approximates your birthday based on the current age input and then uses these rules.

▶
How does my state pension increase?

The state pension has a triple-lock which increases the amount each year by whichever is the highest: prices, earnings or 2.5%

The calculator uses a single assumption for future inflation, the initial default value being 2%.

You can change the assumption for future inflation used by the calculator.

The projected increases are calculated to be the higher of this value and 2.5%

Defined Benefit Pensions
▶
What is a defined benefit pension?

A defined benefit (DB) pension is a type of pension plan that provides a guaranteed income in retirement, usually based on a formula involving factors like your salary and length of service with your employer. Unlike defined contribution pensions, which depend on investment returns, a DB pension provides a predictable retirement income, typically paid for life.

▶
How is my income calculated in a defined benefit pension?

In a DB pension, your retirement income is usually calculated based on your "final salary" (or career average in some schemes) and the number of years you've worked for your employer. For example, a common formula might be a percentage of your final salary for each year of service. This means the more years you work and the higher your salary, the more your pension income will be.

▶
Does my defined benefit pension have a fund?

Generally, defined benefit pensions don’t have individual funds for each member. Instead, there is a central fund managed by the scheme to meet the future pension payments of all members. However, if you leave the scheme, you may have the option to transfer your benefits to a different pension scheme, which would involve calculating a "transfer value" of your accrued benefits.

▶
Can I take tax-free cash with a defined benefit pension?

Yes, many defined benefit pension schemes allow you to take a portion of your pension as a tax-free lump sum when you start receiving your benefits. This may reduce the amount of regular income you receive, as it’s generally exchanged for a smaller pension, but it allows for a one-time, tax-free payment.

▶
What age will I start receiving my defined benefit pension?

The age at which you can start receiving your defined benefit pension is usually set by your scheme’s normal retirement age, often between 60 and 65. Some schemes allow you to take benefits earlier, but this might result in a reduced pension due to early payment. Always check with your scheme to understand your options.

Limitations of the Calculator
▶
What assumptions does the calculator make?
The results of the model are based on deterministic economic assumptions for fund growth and inflation. It is assumed that these are fixed and do not vary from a single value over time. In reality they will fluctuate up and down.

You can adjust the values of the assumptions in the model to see what effect changing them has on your plans. But it is important to note that the results are only a guide to help you understand what might happen under certain scenarios.
▶
How accurate are timings of payments and increases?
The calculator projects values forward based on your age, starting with a full year. It therefore assumes that today is your birthday!

Increases to the state pension occur on April 6th, but the calculator applies the increases at the end of each projection year. Therefore the calculator also assumes your birthday is April 6th.

These approximations are small in relation to the effect of assuming fixed fund growth and inflation values.
▶
Does the calculator constitute financial advice?
The results of the calculator are for information purposes only and do not constitute financial advice. The results should not be relied upon as a sole basis for financial decisions. For personalised financial advice tailored to your individual situation, please consult a qualified financial adviser.
Tax Efficient Drawdown Plan
▶
How does the mix of income affect tax I will need to pay in retirement?

Tax is due on pensions but not ISAs. To minimise tax paid on pensions in the early years of retirement, it is worth using up all tax-free allowances available to you each year. The rest of your income can then consist of ISA withdrawals, if you still have the ISA funds available. The calculator automatically takes this into account when splitting your income in retirement.

▶
What about state pension and any defined benefit pensions I have?

The calculator works out how much State Pension you are due and when it will start to be paid. You can enter the details of a defined benefit pension, and the calculator will add this to the retirement income mix at the appropriate age. Once these kick in, it is likely that your income will exceed your tax-free allowances, so any additional desired income will initially be taken from your ISAs as this will minimise tax due.

▶
How is tax on my pension income calculated?

The calculator determines the tax on your pension income by first applying your personal allowance — the amount you can earn each year without paying tax. It then assesses your total pension income, including the State Pension and any other pensions, and applies the appropriate tax bands (basic, higher, and additional rates) based on your taxable income.

If you choose to take a Tax Free Cash (TFC) lump sum, up to 25% of your pension can be withdrawn tax-free. The calculator also accounts for any remaining TFC percentage not utilised - it works like an extra tax free allowance percentage on all future income taken from your pension.

Additionally, if you select Scottish tax rates, the calculator uses the specific Scottish tax bands to accurately calculate your tax liability.
